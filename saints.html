The Communion of Saints

The Communion of Saints is the spiritual union of all members of the Church — the Church Triumphant (the saints in Heaven), the Church Suffering (the Holy Souls in Purgatory), and the Church Militant (the faithful on earth). These three states of the one Church are united in charity, and they can assist one another:

  • The saints in Heaven intercede for us before God
  • We on earth pray for the Holy Souls in Purgatory and ask the saints for their prayers
  • The Holy Souls, though unable to merit for themselves, can intercede for us (a pious and common belief)

Cf. Apostles' Creed; Council of Trent, Session XXV; Summa Theologica, II-II, q. 83, a. 11.

The Intercession of the Saints

When Catholics "pray to" saints, we are not worshipping them or praying to them as though they were God. We are asking them to pray for us — just as we might ask a friend on earth to pray for us, but with far greater confidence, because the saints are perfected in charity and stand before the throne of God.

Scriptural Basis

  • "And the smoke of the incense of the prayers of the saints ascended up before God from the hand of the angel." — Apocalypse (Revelation) 8:4
  • "The continual prayer of a just man availeth much." — St. James 5:16
  • The wedding at Cana: Our Lady intercedes with her Son, and He responds (St. John 2:1–11)
  • Abraham intercedes for Sodom (Genesis 18:23–33)
  • Moses intercedes for Israel (Exodus 32:11–14)

Patristic Witness

"You say in your book that while we live we are able to pray for each other, but afterwards when we have died, the prayer of no person for another can be heard… But if the Apostles and martyrs while still in the body can pray for others, at a time when they ought still to be anxious for themselves, how much more after their crowns, victories, and triumphs?" — St. Jerome, Against Vigilantius, 6 (A.D. 406)

Cf. Second Council of Nicaea (787); Council of Trent, Session XXV.

The Holy Rosary

The Rosary is the preeminent Marian devotion and one of the most powerful prayers in the Catholic treasury. It consists of the meditation on the mysteries of the life of Christ and His Blessed Mother while reciting vocal prayers (Our Fathers, Hail Marys, and Glory Bes).

The Mysteries

Joyful Mysteries (Monday, Thursday)

  1. The Annunciation (St. Luke 1:26–38)
  2. The Visitation (St. Luke 1:39–56)
  3. The Nativity (St. Luke 2:1–20)
  4. The Presentation in the Temple (St. Luke 2:22–38)
  5. The Finding of the Child Jesus in the Temple (St. Luke 2:41–52)

Sorrowful Mysteries (Tuesday, Friday)

  1. The Agony in the Garden (St. Matthew 26:36–46)
  2. The Scourging at the Pillar (St. John 19:1)
  3. The Crowning with Thorns (St. Matthew 27:27–31)
  4. The Carrying of the Cross (St. John 19:17)
  5. The Crucifixion and Death (St. John 19:18–30)

Glorious Mysteries (Wednesday, Saturday, Sunday)

  1. The Resurrection (St. Mark 16:1–7)
  2. The Ascension (Acts 1:9–11)
  3. The Descent of the Holy Ghost (Acts 2:1–4)
  4. The Assumption of the Blessed Virgin Mary
  5. The Coronation of the Blessed Virgin Mary as Queen of Heaven and Earth

How to Pray the Rosary

  1. Make the Sign of the Cross and pray the Apostles' Creed
  2. Pray one Our Father
  3. Pray three Hail Marys (for faith, hope, and charity)
  4. Pray one Glory Be
  5. Announce the First Mystery, then pray one Our Father, ten Hail Marys, and one Glory Be
  6. Repeat for each of the five mysteries
  7. Conclude with the Hail Holy Queen (Salve Regina)
Salve Regina Hail, holy Queen, Mother of mercy, our life, our sweetness, and our hope. To thee do we cry, poor banished children of Eve. To thee do we send up our sighs, mourning and weeping in this valley of tears. Turn then, most gracious advocate, thine eyes of mercy toward us, and after this our exile, show unto us the blessed fruit of thy womb, Jesus. O clement, O loving, O sweet Virgin Mary.

V. Pray for us, O holy Mother of God.
R. That we may be made worthy of the promises of Christ.

Promises of Our Lady of the Rosary

According to tradition (attributed to Blessed Alan de la Roche), Our Lady made fifteen promises to those who faithfully pray the Rosary, including:

  • Special protection and great graces
  • A powerful armor against hell
  • Growth and flourishing in virtue
  • True devotion to the Rosary is a great sign of predestination
  • Those who recite the Rosary devoutly shall never be conquered by misfortune

Cf. Leo XIII, Supremi Apostolatus Officio (1883) and eleven other Rosary encyclicals; Pius XI, Ingravescentibus Malis (1937).

The Brown Scapular of Our Lady of Mount Carmel

The Brown Scapular is a sacramental consisting of two small pieces of brown cloth connected by strings and worn over the shoulders (one piece on the chest, one on the back). It is the outward sign of consecration to the Blessed Virgin Mary under her title of Our Lady of Mount Carmel.

Origin

On July 16, 1251, the Blessed Virgin appeared to St. Simon Stock, the Prior General of the Carmelite Order, and presented him with the Brown Scapular, saying:

"Receive, My beloved son, this Scapular of thy Order as a badge of my confraternity and for thee and all Carmelites a special sign of grace; whoever dies in this garment will not suffer everlasting fire. It is the sign of salvation, a safeguard in dangers, a pledge of peace and of the covenant."

The Sabbatine Privilege

According to a Papal Bull attributed to Pope John XXII (1322), Our Lady promised that she would descend to Purgatory on the Saturday after the death of those who wear the Brown Scapular and fulfill certain conditions, and bring them to Heaven. The conditions traditionally include:

  • Wearing the Brown Scapular at death
  • Observing chastity according to one's state of life
  • Reciting the Little Office of the Blessed Virgin (or, by dispensation, the daily Rosary)

Enrollment

One must be enrolled (invested) in the Brown Scapular by a priest with the proper faculties. After initial enrollment with the cloth scapular, a blessed Scapular Medal may be worn in its place.

Cf. Pius XII, who called the Scapular the "sign of consecration to the Immaculate Heart of Mary" (1950); numerous papal endorsements.

The Divine Office (Breviarium Romanum)

The Divine Office (Liturgy of the Hours) is the official daily prayer of the Church, sanctifying the hours of the day with psalms, hymns, readings, and prayers. While priests and religious are obliged to pray the Office daily, the laity are warmly encouraged to participate.

The Traditional Hours

  1. Matins — the "Night Office" (traditionally prayed during the night or early morning); the longest Hour, with psalms, lessons, and responsories
  2. Lauds — "Morning Praises," prayed at dawn
  3. Prime — the "First Hour" (approximately 6:00 AM)
  4. Terce — the "Third Hour" (approximately 9:00 AM)
  5. Sext — the "Sixth Hour" (noon)
  6. None — the "Ninth Hour" (approximately 3:00 PM — the hour Christ died)
  7. Vespers — "Evening Prayer" (late afternoon/evening); the most accessible Hour for the laity
  8. Compline — "Night Prayer" (before retiring); a beautiful and brief Hour, excellent for the laity

Cf. Psalm 118:164: "Seven times a day I have given praise to Thee"; St. Pius X, Divino Afflatu (1911).

Sacramentals

Sacramentals are sacred signs or objects instituted by the Church (not directly by Christ, as the sacraments are) that dispose the faithful to receive grace and sanctify various occasions of life.

Common Sacramentals

  • Holy Water — blessed water used in the Sign of the Cross, sprinkled in homes, used at Asperges before Mass. Remits venial sin when used devoutly with the Sign of the Cross.
  • Blessed Candles — especially those blessed at Candlemas (Feb. 2); lit during storms, at the bedside of the dying, during prayer.
  • The Crucifix — a cross bearing the image of the crucified Christ; should be in every Catholic home and room.
  • Medals — the Miraculous Medal, the St. Benedict Medal, and others. Blessed medals carry indulgences and serve as reminders of the saints' intercession.
  • Blessed Salt — used in exorcisms, blessings, and mixed with holy water.
  • The Rosary — itself a sacramental when blessed
  • Blessed Palms — from Palm Sunday; kept in the home
  • The Sign of the Cross — the most fundamental Christian sacramental

How Sacramentals Work

Unlike sacraments, which work ex opere operato (by the very fact of being performed), sacramentals work ex opere operantis Ecclesiae — by the prayer of the Church and the disposition of the one using them. Their efficacy depends partly on the faith and devotion of the user.

Cf. 1917 CIC, Canons 1144–1153; Summa Theologica, III, q. 65, a. 1, ad 6; Roman Catechism, Part II.

Patron Saints

The practice of invoking patron saints — saints who have a special connection to particular occupations, places, illnesses, or states of life — is a beautiful expression of the Communion of Saints.

Notable Patron Saints

  • St. Joseph — Patron of the Universal Church, of workers, of a happy death, of fathers
  • St. Michael the Archangel — Patron of soldiers, police, the sick, and the dying; protector of the Church
  • St. Thomas Aquinas — Patron of students, universities, and theologians
  • St. Thérèse of Lisieux — Patron of missionaries, florists; Doctor of the Church
  • St. Anthony of Padua — Patron of lost things; Doctor of the Church
  • St. Jude Thaddeus — Patron of desperate and impossible causes
  • St. Philomena — Patroness of the living Rosary, of infants, of youth
  • St. Padre Pio — Patron of civil defense volunteers and stress relief
  • St. Monica — Patroness of mothers, especially those praying for wayward children
  • St. Augustine — Patron of theologians, printers, and brewers; Doctor of the Church
  • St. Cecilia — Patroness of musicians and Church music
  • St. Luke the Evangelist — Patron of physicians, surgeons, and artists
  • St. Francis of Assisi — Patron of animals, ecology, and merchants
  • St. Rita of Cascia — Patroness of impossible causes and abused wives

Litanies

A litany is a form of responsive prayer in which a series of invocations or petitions are followed by a fixed response (e.g., "Pray for us" or "Have mercy on us"). The Church has approved several litanies for public use:

Approved Litanies

  1. Litany of the Saints — the most ancient, used in solemn liturgical functions (ordinations, Rogation Days, etc.)
  2. Litany of the Blessed Virgin Mary (Litany of Loreto) — invoking Our Lady under her many beautiful titles
  3. Litany of the Holy Name of Jesus
  4. Litany of the Sacred Heart of Jesus
  5. Litany of St. Joseph
  6. Litany of the Precious Blood
